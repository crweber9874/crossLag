The Cross Lagged Panel Model

  • The CLPM is common in many social science applications, particularly in longitudinal, observational research designs.

  • The design is straightforward, motivated by the difficulty in figuring out whether \(X \rightarrow Y\), \(Y \rightarrow X\)

  • Political science: (Federico and Gole De Zavala 2023; Craemer 2008; Claasen 2008; Hatemi, Crabtree and Smith 2019; Hetherington and Globeti 2002; Luttig 2021, Klandermans 2004; Lupu 2015; Goren and Chapp 2017; Layman and Carsey 2002; Putz 2002; Tilley, Neundor and Hobolt 2018; Sibley and Duckitt 2010, among many others)

Political Psychology, Personality and Politics

  • We focus here primarily on its applicability in political psychology

  • Personality and Politics: Personality traits are endogenous to politics. Politics influences personality (Luttig 2021; Bakker, Lelkes, and Malke 2021).

  • Luttig (2021): Party evaluations predict authoritarianism; but authoritarianism does not predict party evaluations

  • Reciprocal effects (Bakker, Lelkes, and Malka 2021)

Weber and Federico (2007)

  • Authoritarianism stems from anxious and avoidant psychological attachment patterns

Although we assume that general psychological orientations like anxiety and avoidance should be causally prior to worldviews and ideology—like other researchers in this area (e.g., Jost et al., 2003)—longitudinal data would provide a less-ambiguous pattern of support for this assumption. For example, panel data on all relevant constructs would allow us to examine the strength of competing causal patterns using a cross-lagged design (Finkel, 1995). Similarly, actual developmental data might allow us to see whether attachment patterns early in life are predictive of different worldviews and ideologies later on.

The Model

Complicated Effects

  • Only under restrictive conditions will the CLPM retrieve the correct causal effects.

  • The model is in fact a combination of direct and indirect effects, where unobserved confounds have the potential to influence results.

Complicated Effects

\[ y_{t, i}=a_0 + a_1 y_{t-1, i} + a_2 x_{t-1, i} + e_{1,i} \\ x_{t, i}=b_0 + b_1 y_{t-1, i} + b_2 x_{t-1, i} + e_{2,i} \]

Quickly: The Logic

  • The design leverages the temporal ordering of measurements in a panel data set.

  • If \(X\) is measured at time \(t-1\) and \(t\), and \(Y\) is measured at time \(t-1\) and \(t\), then one can estimate a model that predicts \(Y_{t}\) from \(X_{t-1}\) and \(Y_{t-1}\)

  • The CLPM is a variant of the Autoregressive Distributed Lag (ADL) model

  • The CLPM is correlational model, not a causal model.

  • Granger Causality: \(X\) Granger causes \(Y\) if \(X_{t-1}\) predicts \(Y_{t}\) better than \(Y_{t-1}\)

Identifying the Causal Effect of \(X\) on \(Y\)

  • A linear model with lagged variables \[ y_{t, i}=a_0 + a_1 y_{t-1, i} + a_2 x_{t-1, i} + e_{1,i} \\ x_{t, i}=b_0 + b_1 y_{t-1, i} + b_2 x_{t-1, i} + e_{2,i} \]
  • The potential outcomes framework and the ignorability assumption (Hernán and Robins 2020; Vanderweele 2015)

The Ignorability Assumption

  • Or, No Unmeasured Confounding, No Selection on Observables, No Selection Bias

  • A strong assumption in panel designs.

\[ Y_{t} (X) \perp X_{E} | Y_{h}, X_{h}, V_{h}, C, U \]

  • \(Y_{h}\), \(X_{h}\), \(V_{h}\) are the histories of \(x\), \(y\), and time-varying covariates, \(v\).

  • Time invariant controls, \(C\) and unmeasured confounders, \(U\).

What it Means

  • The potential outcomes are conditionally independent of exposure, given the history of of X, Y, time-varying covariables, V, time-stable controls, and unmeasured confounders, U.

  • If ignorability holds, then \(\tau_{x}\) – the treatment effect of X on Y – can be estimated from \(a_1\) and \(b_1\).

The CLPM and Unmeasured Confounding

  • The cross lag correlation, and comparing the size of cross lagged correlations to approximate a causal effect, is fraught with problems.

  • Stable, individual differences in the variables can lead to spurious correlations.

  • The Random Intercept Cross Lagged Panel Regression Model (Hamaker, Kuiper, and Grasman 2015)

Using Simulations to Illustrate the Problem

  • How effectively does the CLPM retrieve the correct parameter estimates?

  • We simulated data under the RI-CLPM. We only varied the amount of stability in x and y \([-.75, -0.25, 0, 0.25, 0.75]\)

  • Other characteristics are fixed. The sample size is 1,000. The cross-lagged effect of x on y is 0. The variances of p and q are 1, and the covariance is 0.5.

  • 1,000 datasets were specified for \(5^5 = 25\) different combinations of x and y stability

The Model

  • For each dataset, we estimated a linear regression model:

\[ y_{t, i}=a_0 + a_1 y_{t-1, i} + a_2 x_{t-1, i} + e_{1,i} \]

  • Theoretically, \(a_2\) should be zero, as this is what we specified.

  • Steps

  1. Estimate the model

  2. Extract the coefficients for \(a_2\) and \(b_2\)

  3. Save the results

The CLPM Retrieves the Wrong Estimates

  • Vary the stability of \(y_t\) fixing the stability across \(x\) at 0. Autoregression in \(y\) but not \(x\)

Except when \(y\) is Unstable

  • Vary the stability of \(x_t\) fixing the stability across \(y\) at 0. There is an autoregressive effect of \(x_t\).

Jointly vary the stability of both \(x\) and \(y\)

  • Autoregression in \(y\) yields the wrong estimate for \(x_{t-1}\).

The CLPM, Personality and Politics

  • Bakker, Lelkes, and Malka (2021) fit a CLPM in a comparative public opinion project.

  • Personality predicts politics: In the Netherlands, politics predicts personality. Personality characteristics predict attitudes towards the EU, Euthanasia, Ideology, Immigration, and Income.

  • Politics predicts personality: In the Netherlands, these same policies predict personality.

  • Similar findings in a German longitudinal panel (GESIS); reciprocal relationships.

  • Similar findings in the U.S. General Social Survey. Abortion, ideology, LGBT attitudes, and social welfare preferences predict authoritarianism, and vice-versa.

Concluding Remarks

  • Identifying causal estimands using different SEM DAGs.

  • Carefully consider confounding and estimation using Hernan and Robins (2020) and Vanderweele (2015) g-formula and g-estimation.

  • Direct, indirect, and total effects in these models.

  • Caution: Stable, unit level effects can lead to spurious estimates when using the CLPM.

  • In psychology examples, where characteristics like authoritarianism – which are partially heritable – are quite stable over time, the CLPM may produce distorted estimates.
